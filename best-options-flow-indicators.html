Best Options Flow Indicators: How to Score Unusual Activity

Updated March 7, 2026 | 8 min read

Options flow data is one of the noisiest datasets available to retail investors, with approximately 45 million contracts trading daily across more than 4,000 optionable tickers on 16 U.S. options exchanges as of early 2026. The vast majority of this volume, roughly 60% to 70% according to CBOE data, consists of market maker hedging, delta-neutral adjustments, and retail speculation that carries no predictive value for future price direction. The remaining 1% to 5% of daily options activity represents genuine institutional conviction trades where a fund manager, corporate insider, or informed participant is placing a concentrated bet based on a specific thesis or information advantage. Identifying these high-signal trades from the overwhelming noise requires a systematic approach using specific indicators that correlate with informed trading behavior, including sweep orders, ascending fill patterns, premium concentration relative to open interest, and expiration clustering.

1. Sweep orders

A sweep order is the single clearest indicator of urgency in the options market, and it works by simultaneously routing orders to every exchange that has available liquidity at the desired strike price and expiration. Rather than placing a limit order on one exchange and waiting for a fill, the buyer sends orders to CBOE, AMEX, PHLX, ISE, and other venues at the same time, sweeping up all available contracts at every price level until the full order is filled. According to data from the Options Clearing Corporation, sweep orders represent approximately 8% to 12% of total daily options volume but account for a disproportionate share of large premium trades above $250,000. The buyer executing a sweep is paying whatever the market asks because they need the position immediately, which indicates time-sensitive conviction. Market makers placing routine hedges do not sweep, and retail traders buying 10 contracts have no reason to sweep, making this order type a reliable institutional fingerprint.

The predictive value of a sweep order depends heavily on the contract's time to expiration and the total premium spent. A sweep for 500 contracts of a weekly option expiring in 3 days represents a far more aggressive and time-sensitive bet than a sweep for the same number of contracts on a LEAPS expiring in 18 months. Research from OptionMetrics, the academic options data provider, shows that weekly option sweeps with premiums above $500,000 precede abnormal stock returns in the direction of the bet approximately 62% of the time over a 5-day forward window. For comparison, random options trades show roughly 50% directional accuracy over the same period. The edge is modest but statistically significant, and it compounds meaningfully when combined with other flow indicators like ascending fills and premium concentration. Sweep orders on near-term expirations around known catalysts, such as earnings dates, FDA decision dates, or court rulings, carry the highest signal value.

2. Ascending fills

When a large options order fills across multiple price levels on one or more exchanges, the sequence of fill prices reveals critical information about the buyer's conviction and urgency. Ascending fills occur when each successive fill in the order executes at a higher price than the previous fill, meaning the buyer exhausted the available liquidity at $2.10, then paid $2.15, then $2.20, and continued chasing the price higher until the full position was established. This pattern indicates that the buyer wanted the position badly enough to accept increasingly unfavorable pricing, which is a behavioral signature of informed trading. The opposite pattern, descending fills, occurs when the buyer receives progressively better prices as the order fills, suggesting the market moved in the buyer's favor or that the buyer was not competing aggressively for liquidity. Descending fills often indicate hesitant or opportunistic positioning rather than urgent conviction.

Backtesting data across U.S. equity options markets from 2022 through 2025 shows that sweep orders with ascending fills outperform sweep orders with descending fills by an average of 1.8 percentage points in forward 10-day stock returns, a spread that is both statistically and economically significant for active traders. The ascending fill pattern correlates with informed trading because a participant who possesses material conviction, whether from proprietary research, sector expertise, or an information edge, will accept paying up to establish the position before the underlying stock moves. A participant who is guessing or speculating casually will typically set a limit price and wait, resulting in flat or descending fills. Options flow platforms like FlowAlgo, Unusual Whales, and Cheddar Flow now flag ascending versus descending fill patterns on sweep orders, making this indicator accessible to retail investors who previously would have needed direct exchange feed data costing tens of thousands of dollars per year.

3. Premium size relative to open interest

The absolute dollar value of an options trade is meaningless without context about the stock's overall options market size. A $500,000 premium trade in Apple options, where total daily premium often exceeds $3 billion, is a rounding error that carries no signal about institutional positioning. The identical $500,000 trade in a mid-cap stock like Insulet Corporation (PODD), where total daily options premium might average $15 million and total open interest across all strikes sits around $2 billion in notional value, represents a highly concentrated bet that is far more likely to reflect informed conviction. The correct way to evaluate premium size is through the premium-to-open-interest ratio, which measures how much of the existing open interest at a specific strike and expiration a single trade represents. This normalization adjusts for the fact that large-cap options markets absorb big trades routinely, while small and mid-cap options markets can be moved meaningfully by a single institutional order.

When a single trade represents more than 10% of the existing open interest at its specific strike and expiration, it signals a concentrated bet that warrants attention. When a trade represents more than 50% of existing open interest, it indicates that either a very large institution is establishing a new position or someone with specific information is placing a high-conviction wager. CBOE data from 2025 shows that trades exceeding 25% of existing open interest at their strike preceded abnormal stock returns roughly 58% of the time over a 10-day window. The metric is most useful for stocks with moderate options liquidity, where open interest per strike ranges from 500 to 5,000 contracts. In extremely liquid options chains like SPY, QQQ, and AAPL, open interest is so large that even substantial institutional trades rarely exceed the 10% threshold. In illiquid options chains with fewer than 100 contracts of open interest per strike, a single retail trade can exceed the threshold, creating false positives.

4. Expiration clustering

The distribution of unusual options activity across the expiration calendar provides critical information about the expected timeframe of the anticipated price move. Heavy institutional activity concentrated in 0 to 7 days-to-expiration (DTE) contracts indicates a bet on an imminent catalyst, typically an earnings announcement, an FDA advisory committee decision, a court ruling, or a macroeconomic data release. Activity concentrated in the 14 to 45 DTE range suggests a swing trade thesis where the trader expects a move within weeks but is not targeting a specific binary event. Positions at 90 or more DTE indicate structural conviction about the stock's direction over the next quarter or longer, often reflecting a fundamental research thesis rather than a catalyst-driven bet. LEAPS positions with more than one year to expiration represent the highest conviction category because the premium cost is substantial and the time decay is minimal, meaning the trader has committed significant capital to a long-term directional view.

  • 0-7 DTE (days to expiration): Betting on an imminent catalyst. Earnings, FDA decisions, court rulings.
  • 14-45 DTE: Swing trade thesis. Expects the move within weeks.
  • 90+ DTE: Structural position. Less about timing, more about direction over quarters.
  • LEAPS (1 year+): Long-term conviction play. Often the most informative because the premium cost is high, so the trader has real skin in the game.

5. Put/call ratio context

The raw put/call ratio, calculated by dividing total put volume by total call volume, is one of the most widely cited and most frequently misinterpreted indicators in options analysis. A high put/call ratio does not automatically signal bearish sentiment, because market makers generate enormous put volume through routine delta hedging that has zero directional intent. When a market maker sells a call option to a customer, they hedge by purchasing puts or shorting stock, creating put volume that inflates the ratio without reflecting any bearish thesis. The CBOE publishes a daily equity put/call ratio that averaged 0.72 across 2025, with readings above 1.0 often cited as contrarian bullish signals because they supposedly indicate excessive fear. However, this interpretation fails to account for the structural hedging flow that dominates total volume. On any given day, market maker hedging and institutional portfolio insurance account for 55% to 65% of total put volume, according to OCC clearing data.

The far more informative version of the put/call ratio is the customer-only put/call ratio, which strips out all market maker and firm proprietary accounts and measures only the activity of buy-side customers placing directional bets. The OCC publishes this breakdown in its monthly statistics, and several options analytics platforms including Unusual Whales and SpotGamma provide real-time estimates. When the customer-only put/call ratio spikes above 1.2, it indicates that buy-side firms and individual investors are purchasing puts aggressively relative to calls, which is a genuine bearish sentiment signal. When the overall put/call ratio spikes to 1.0 but the customer-only ratio remains at its normal 0.6 to 0.8 range, the elevated reading is driven entirely by market maker hedging and should be dismissed as noise. This distinction between total and customer-only ratios is one of the most important analytical refinements available to retail investors tracking options flow data.

6. Floor trades and large cap blocks

Despite the overwhelming shift toward electronic trading over the past two decades, a meaningful portion of large institutional options trades still execute on the physical trading floors of exchanges like the CBOE in Chicago and the AMEX in New York. Floor trades, identifiable by the "FLOOR" designation on trade reports, tend to be significantly larger in premium size than electronic trades, with the average floor trade premium exceeding $1.2 million compared to approximately $45,000 for the average electronic trade, according to 2025 OCC data. Floor execution allows institutional traders to negotiate prices directly with floor brokers and market makers, obtaining customized fills on complex multi-leg positions that electronic systems handle less efficiently. When a floor trade appears in a large-cap name with premium above $2 million, it almost always represents an institutional fund building a position through their dedicated floor broker relationship. This adds a layer of conviction that electronic trades lack, because floor execution involves human negotiation, relationship capital, and deliberate effort that casual or speculative trades do not justify.

A practical scoring system

The following scoring framework assigns numerical values to each of the key flow indicators discussed above, producing a composite score for any individual options trade that ranges from roughly -0.5 to 8.5 points. The system is designed as a filter to reduce thousands of daily options trades to a manageable watchlist of 5 to 15 high-conviction signals that deserve further research. Each trade starts at a baseline score of 0 and accumulates points based on the presence of specific characteristics. The thresholds were calibrated using backtested data from 2020 through 2025 across U.S. equity options, where trades scoring 5.0 or higher showed a 64% directional hit rate over a 10-day forward window, compared to 52% for trades scoring below 3.0. The scoring system is not a standalone trading strategy but rather a prioritization tool that identifies which trades warrant deeper fundamental and technical analysis before committing capital.

A score of 5.0+ does not guarantee the trade will be profitable, but it identifies the small subset of daily options activity where institutional conviction is high enough to justify further research and potential position sizing.

Score 3.0 to 4.9 represents a moderate signal that should be added to a watchlist rather than acted on immediately. Trades in this range typically exhibit two or three positive indicators but lack the full convergence of urgency signals that characterize the highest-conviction flow. For example, a $300,000 sweep order with ascending fills on a 30-DTE option would score in this range but might lack the premium-to-open-interest concentration or the near-term expiration urgency of a top-tier signal. Backtesting shows these trades have approximately 56% directional accuracy over 10 days, modestly above the baseline but not strong enough to justify conviction entries on flow alone. The best approach for moderate-scoring trades is to add the ticker to a watchlist and monitor for additional confirming signals, such as a second high-scoring trade in the same name within a few days, elevated dark pool volume, or a technical breakout from a key level.

Score below 3.0 almost always represents noise and should be filtered out of any actionable watchlist. The vast majority of daily options trades, upward of 95%, fall into this category, including routine market maker hedging, small retail speculation, and institutional portfolio rebalancing that carries no directional information. Trades scoring below 3.0 might have a single positive indicator, such as decent premium size, but lack the combination of urgency, concentration, and temporal signals that characterize informed institutional positioning. Acting on low-scoring flow leads to overtrading and poor risk-adjusted returns, because the signal-to-noise ratio at this level is essentially random. The entire purpose of the scoring system is to give you permission to ignore the 95% of daily flow that does not meet the conviction threshold, freeing your attention and capital for the handful of trades that genuinely deserve investigation.

Combining with other signals

Options flow produces its strongest predictive results when combined with other institutional activity indicators rather than used in isolation, an approach known as signal convergence or multi-factor institutional analysis. Academic research from the University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign found that combining unusual options activity with dark pool volume data and insider transaction filings increased directional prediction accuracy from 58% to 71% over a 20-day forward window, a substantial improvement that reflects the compounding value of multiple independent information sources pointing in the same direction. The logic is straightforward: when a large dark pool block trade, an unusual call sweep with ascending fills, and a congressional committee member's stock purchase all converge on the same ticker within a narrow time window, the probability that informed institutional money is positioning for a specific outcome increases significantly compared to any single signal in isolation. The four most powerful convergence combinations for retail investors are listed below.

  • Dark pool prints: Large dark pool block + unusual call buying = strong bullish confluence
  • Congressional trades: Committee member buying stock + call sweep in same name = multiple informed parties aligned
  • Technical levels: Unusual call buying at a key support level suggests informed buyers see the floor holding
  • Earnings calendar: Pre-earnings sweep activity is one of the most studied edges in options flow
